# Swing Trading Glossary

A comprehensive reference of swing trading terminology. Each term is defined in the context of daily-timeframe swing trading (holding periods of 2-30 days).

---

### ATR (Average True Range)

A volatility indicator that measures the average range of price movement over a specified period, typically 14 days. ATR accounts for gaps by using the greater of: current high minus current low, absolute value of current high minus previous close, or absolute value of current low minus previous close. Swing traders use ATR to set stop losses and profit targets as multiples of ATR (e.g., 1.5x ATR stop).

### Anchored VWAP

Volume-Weighted Average Price anchored to a specific date or event, such as an earnings report, IPO, or swing low. Unlike rolling VWAP, anchored VWAP provides a cumulative average from a meaningful starting point. Institutional traders use anchored VWAP as a reference for fair value, making it a strong support/resistance level.

### Backtest

The process of applying a trading strategy to historical data to evaluate its performance. A valid backtest uses out-of-sample data, accounts for slippage and commissions, and avoids look-ahead bias. Walk-forward backtesting splits data into training and testing windows to simulate real-world conditions.

### Bollinger Bands

A volatility envelope consisting of a 20-period SMA (middle band) plus and minus 2 standard deviations. When bands contract (a "squeeze"), a breakout in either direction becomes more likely. Price touching the lower band in an uptrend can signal a pullback buying opportunity; touching the upper band in a downtrend can signal a short entry.

### Breakout

A price move above a defined resistance level, typically accompanied by increased volume. Valid breakouts show conviction through volume expansion (40%+ above average) and follow-through in subsequent sessions. Failed breakouts that reverse back below the breakout level often lead to sharp moves in the opposite direction.

### Confirmation Candle

A candlestick that validates a setup signal. For bullish setups, this is typically a candle that closes above the prior candle's high with above-average volume. For bearish setups, it closes below the prior candle's low. Entering before confirmation increases the risk of false signals.

### Cup and Handle

A bullish continuation pattern identified by William O'Neil. The "cup" is a U-shaped consolidation (12-35% depth, 3-6+ weeks duration), followed by a tight pullback called the "handle" (less than 12% depth, in the upper portion of the cup). The breakout above the handle high on volume signals the continuation of the prior uptrend.

### Divergence

A condition where price makes a new high or low but a momentum indicator (RSI, MACD) does not confirm. Bearish divergence occurs when price makes a higher high but RSI makes a lower high, suggesting weakening momentum. Bullish divergence is the inverse. Triple divergence (three consecutive divergent swings) carries a higher probability of reversal.

### EMA (Exponential Moving Average)

A moving average that applies greater weight to recent prices, making it more responsive to new data than an SMA. The EMA9 and EMA20 are commonly used to define the "pullback zone" in trend-following strategies. A stack of EMA9 > EMA20 > SMA50 confirms an established uptrend.

### Engulfing Pattern

A two-candle reversal pattern where the second candle's body completely engulfs the first candle's body. A bullish engulfing at support suggests buyers have overwhelmed sellers. A bearish engulfing at resistance suggests the opposite. The pattern is more significant when accompanied by above-average volume.

### Flag Pattern

A short consolidation that forms a rectangular channel sloping against the prior trend. A bull flag slopes downward after a strong advance; a bear flag slopes upward after a sharp decline. Flags typically resolve in the direction of the prior trend. Volume should decline during the flag and expand on the breakout.

### Gap (Opening Gap)

A price discontinuity between the prior close and the current open. Gaps are classified as breakaway (starting a new trend), runaway (continuing a trend), or exhaustion (ending a trend). Institutional gaps on high volume often act as support/resistance zones. Intraday gap-fill levels are closely watched by swing traders for re-entry opportunities.

### Hammer / Shooting Star

Single-candle reversal patterns. A hammer forms at the bottom of a downtrend with a long lower wick and small body near the top, indicating buyers absorbed selling pressure. A shooting star forms at the top of an uptrend with a long upper wick and small body near the bottom, indicating sellers rejected higher prices.

### Institutional Volume

Trading volume that exceeds 150-200% of the 50-day average, suggesting participation by mutual funds, hedge funds, or other large players. Institutional volume on a breakout validates the move. Institutional volume on a reversal candle warns of distribution. Volume is the footprint of institutional activity.

### Market Regime

The prevailing market condition that determines which strategies have the highest probability of success. The five regimes are: Trending Up, Trending Down, Ranging, High Volatility, and Transitioning. Regime is determined by SMA slopes, ADX readings, market breadth, and VIX levels. Selecting the right strategy for the current regime is more important than strategy selection itself.

### Measured Move

A price projection technique that estimates the target of a breakout by adding the height of the base (or prior move) to the breakout point. For a flag pattern, the measured move equals the length of the flagpole projected from the breakout level. Measured moves provide data-driven profit targets.

### Momentum

The rate of acceleration of a security's price. Momentum indicators (RSI, MACD, rate of change) measure whether price movement is accelerating or decelerating. Momentum strategies buy strength and sell weakness. Mean-reversion strategies do the opposite, buying when momentum is temporarily oversold.

### Moving Average Stack

A configuration where shorter-period moving averages are above longer-period ones (e.g., EMA9 > EMA20 > SMA50 > SMA200), confirming a strong uptrend. An inverted stack (EMA9 < EMA20 < SMA50) confirms a downtrend. The stack is a prerequisite for many trend-following strategies.

### Pivot Point

A significant swing high or low that represents a point where price changed direction. Pivot highs act as resistance on the next rally; pivot lows act as support on the next decline. Breakouts above pivot highs are entry triggers for momentum strategies like VCP Breakout.

### Position Sizing

The process of determining how many shares to buy based on account risk parameters. The standard formula is: Position Size = Risk Amount / (Entry Price - Stop Price). Proper position sizing ensures that no single trade can cause catastrophic damage to an account.

### R-Multiple

The ratio of profit or loss to the initial risk (R) on a trade. If risk is $2/share and the trade profits $6/share, the R-multiple is 3R. R-multiples allow comparison across trades regardless of share price or position size. A strategy with an average R-multiple above 1.0 and a reasonable win rate is profitable over time.

### Relative Strength (RS) Rank

A percentile ranking (0-99) of a stock's price performance relative to all other stocks in a universe over a defined period. An RS Rank of 90 means the stock has outperformed 90% of its peers. RS leaders in uptrending regimes have a statistical edge for continuation. RS laggards in downtrends are short candidates.

### RSI (Relative Strength Index)

A momentum oscillator ranging from 0 to 100, calculated from the ratio of average gains to average losses over a period (typically 14 days). RSI below 30 is considered oversold; above 70 is overbought. RSI(5) is used for short-term mean-reversion signals. RSI(14) is used for trend assessment.

### Resistance

A price level where selling pressure has historically prevented further advances. Resistance forms at prior swing highs, round numbers, moving averages, and VWAP levels. A breakout above resistance on volume converts it to support. Multiple tests of resistance without a breakout often lead to an eventual breakdown.

### Risk-Reward Ratio

The ratio of potential loss to potential gain on a trade. A 1:3 risk-reward ratio means risking $1 to potentially make $3. A minimum 1:2 risk-reward ratio is a standard swing trading filter. The risk-reward ratio, combined with win rate, determines the expected value of a strategy.

### SMA (Simple Moving Average)

The arithmetic mean of closing prices over a specified period. SMA50 and SMA200 are the most widely watched by institutional traders. The "golden cross" (SMA50 crossing above SMA200) and "death cross" (SMA50 crossing below SMA200) are regime signals. Price relative to SMA200 is a primary trend filter.

### Stage Analysis

Stan Weinstein's framework that classifies a stock's lifecycle into four stages: Stage 1 (basing), Stage 2 (advancing), Stage 3 (topping), and Stage 4 (declining). Swing traders focus on Stage 2 breakouts for long entries and Stage 4 breakdowns for short entries. The 30-week moving average (approximately SMA150) determines the current stage.

### Stop Loss

A predetermined price level at which a losing trade is exited to limit risk. Fixed stops are set at a specific price (often ATR-based). Trailing stops move with price to lock in profits. A stop loss must be placed before entry and honored without exception.

### Support

A price level where buying pressure has historically prevented further declines. Support forms at prior swing lows, round numbers, moving averages, and VWAP levels. A breakdown below support on volume converts it to resistance. Support levels are used to place stop losses just below them.

### Swing High / Swing Low

A swing high is a candlestick high that is higher than the highs of the candles immediately before and after it. A swing low is a candlestick low that is lower than the lows of the candles immediately before and after it. These pivots define the trend structure: higher swing highs and higher swing lows confirm an uptrend.

### Trailing Stop

A stop loss that moves in the direction of the trade to lock in profits. Common methods include: ATR-based (e.g., 1.5x ATR below price), percentage-based (e.g., 5% from highest close), and swing-low based (below the most recent swing low). The trailing method should match the strategy's volatility profile.

### VCP (Volatility Contraction Pattern)

Mark Minervini's signature pattern characterized by a series of contracting price ranges (tightening pivots) on declining volume. Each contraction represents sellers drying up while buyers absorb supply. The breakout above the final contraction on 40%+ above-average volume is the entry trigger. VCP is most effective in strongly trending markets.

### VWAP (Volume-Weighted Average Price)

The average price weighted by volume, representing the true average price at which shares traded during a session. Institutional traders use VWAP as a benchmark for execution quality. A VWAP reclaim (price crossing back above VWAP after trading below it) is a bullish intraday signal. Anchored VWAP extends this concept across multiple sessions.

### Volume Confirmation

The principle that significant price moves should be accompanied by above-average volume to be considered valid. Breakouts on low volume are suspect. Pullbacks on declining volume are healthy. Volume spikes on reversal candles indicate institutional participation. Volume is the single most important confirmation tool in swing trading.

### Williams %R

A momentum oscillator ranging from -100 to 0, measuring a stock's closing price relative to its high-low range over a period (typically 14 days). Values above -20 indicate overbought conditions; below -80 indicate oversold conditions. Williams %R is mathematically related to the Stochastic Oscillator and is used for mean-reversion timing.
