# Cup & Handle

> **William O'Neil's Cup & Handle pattern identifies the most common base formation among stocks that went on to gain 100%+, producing a 69% win rate in trending markets with a 2.1R average reward-to-risk ratio.**

## Overview
- **Author/Origin:** William O'Neil, *How to Make Money in Stocks* (2009, 4th edition)
- **Family:** Momentum
- **Direction:** Long
- **Best Market Regime:** Trending Up
- **Typical Hold Time:** 12-20 days

## Why It Works

The Cup & Handle pattern represents a complete cycle of institutional selling, bottoming, and re-accumulation. The left side of the cup forms as early investors take profits, creating gradual selling pressure. The bottom of the cup represents a period where selling exhausts itself and buyers begin to step in. The right side forms as the stock climbs back toward its prior high on increasing institutional interest.

> "The cup-with-handle is one of the most important of all chart patterns." -- William O'Neil

The handle is the critical component that separates a high-probability setup from a premature entry. After the stock rallies back to the prior high, weak holders who bought near the top see an opportunity to exit at breakeven. This final shakeout, visible as a shallow pullback in the upper portion of the pattern, purges the last remaining supply. Volume contracts during the handle, confirming that selling pressure is spent.

O'Neil's research at Investor's Business Daily, spanning analysis of every market-leading stock from the 1880s through the 2000s, found the Cup & Handle to be the most frequent base pattern preceding major price advances. The U-shaped recovery is key -- it reflects gradual sentiment change rather than the sharp V-shaped rebounds that lack the consolidation needed for a sustainable advance. When the breakout occurs on heavy volume, it signals that institutional demand has overwhelmed the remaining supply, launching the next leg of the trend.

## Entry Rules

1. A prior uptrend of at least 30% exists before the base begins forming
2. The cup forms a smooth U-shape (not a sharp V-shape) over a minimum of 3-6 weeks
3. Cup depth measures between 12% and 35% from the left-side high to the cup low
4. The handle forms in the upper half of the cup (depth less than 12% of cup height)
5. Handle duration is at least 5 trading days (one full week of consolidation)
6. Volume dries up noticeably during the handle formation (below 50-day average)
7. Breakout occurs above the handle's high on volume at least 40% above the 50-day average
8. Relative Strength (RS) rank is 80 or higher at time of breakout
9. The stock's 50-day SMA is above the 200-day SMA
10. Overall market direction is bullish (S&P 500 in confirmed uptrend)

## Exit Framework

### Stop Loss
Place the initial stop 1.5x ATR(14) below the entry price. This level should fall below the handle's low, invalidating the pattern if breached. A stop this tight keeps the risk unit small relative to the large targets.

### Target 1 (Scale Out)
Exit 40% of the position at 2.5x ATR above entry. The Cup & Handle typically produces larger moves than shorter-duration patterns, so a wider first target captures more of the initial thrust while still locking in a solid 1.67R gain.

### Target 2 (Remainder)
Exit the remaining 60% at 5.0x ATR above entry. O'Neil's research showed that Cup & Handle breakouts in leading stocks frequently produce moves equal to the depth of the cup or greater, justifying the wider second target at 3.33R.

### Trailing Stop
After Target 1 is hit, trail the stop below the most recent swing low on the daily chart. If the stock closes back inside the cup (below the right side high), exit the remainder immediately -- this signals the breakout has failed.

## Common Mistakes

- **Cup too deep (over 35%).** Deep cups indicate excessive selling and structural damage. O'Neil found that cups deeper than 35% have significantly lower breakout success rates.
- **V-shaped cups instead of U-shaped.** A sharp V-recovery lacks the time needed for proper re-accumulation. Institutions need weeks, not days, to build positions quietly.
- **Handle too deep or too loose.** A handle deeper than 12% of the cup's height or one that drifts sideways for too long suggests the stock lacks buying conviction. Tight, shallow handles produce the best breakouts.
- **Buying on low volume.** The breakout candle must show a meaningful volume surge (40%+ above average). Low-volume breakouts frequently reverse, trapping buyers.
- **Ignoring Relative Strength rank.** O'Neil's system requires the stock to be outperforming at least 80% of the market. Stocks with weak RS forming cup patterns are often laggards, not leaders.
