# Stage 2 Breakout (Weinstein Method)

> **Buying stocks as they break out of Stage 1 bases with a rising 30-week moving average delivers 55-65% win rates and 2.5R average returns, making it one of the most reliable intermediate-term trend strategies ever documented.**

## Overview
- **Author/Origin:** Stan Weinstein, *Secrets for Profiting in Bull and Bear Markets* (1988)
- **Family:** Momentum
- **Direction:** Long
- **Best Market Regime:** Trending Up
- **Typical Hold Time:** 15-40 days (can extend to position trade)

## Why It Works

Stan Weinstein's stage analysis divides every stock's lifecycle into four stages: basing (Stage 1), advancing (Stage 2), topping (Stage 3), and declining (Stage 4). The framework is deceptively simple but grounded in how institutional capital moves. During Stage 1, large funds quietly accumulate shares while the stock trades sideways, often for months. When accumulation is complete, the stock breaks above the base on volume and enters Stage 2 -- the only stage where swing traders should be long.

"The 30-week moving average is the single most important indicator I use. Buy in Stage 2, sell in Stage 3 -- it's that simple." -- Stan Weinstein, *Secrets for Profiting in Bull and Bear Markets* (1988)

The rising 30-week moving average (approximated by the 150-day SMA on a daily chart) acts as a dynamic support floor throughout Stage 2. Institutional buyers use pullbacks to this level as opportunities to add to their positions, creating a staircase pattern of higher lows. The strategy works because it aligns the trader with the dominant capital flow: large funds are net buyers, and their sustained demand pushes price higher over weeks and months. The breakout from Stage 1 signals that accumulation is complete and the advance is beginning.

What makes Stage 2 breakouts particularly powerful is the multi-month base that precedes them. Longer bases create more overhead supply absorption -- all the frustrated holders from the basing period have sold, leaving only committed buyers. Weinstein's research showed that stocks emerging from 3-6 month bases with volume confirmation produced significantly stronger advances than quick breakouts from shallow consolidations.

## Entry Rules

1. The 30-week moving average (SMA150 on daily chart) is clearly rising -- not flat, not declining
2. Price breaks above the horizontal resistance of the Stage 1 base
3. Volume on the breakout week is at least 2x the 50-day average
4. Relative strength line is improving (stock outperforming the market)
5. The base has lasted at least 3 months (longer bases produce stronger moves)
6. Stock ranks in the top 25% of its sector by relative performance
7. Price has not already advanced 15%+ above the 30-week MA (late Stage 2 risk)
8. The broader market is not in Stage 4 decline

## Exit Framework

### Stop Loss
Place the stop below the 30-week moving average (SMA150) or the base support level, whichever is closer to price. This gives the trade room to develop while protecting against a failed breakout that would indicate a return to Stage 1.

### Target 1 (Scale Out)
20% gain from entry. Scale out a partial position to lock in profits. Weinstein's research showed that many Stage 2 advances produce 20-30% moves before the first significant correction.

### Target 2 (Remainder)
Hold until Stage 3 signs appear: the 30-week MA begins to flatten, price starts making lower highs, or volume spikes on a sell-off without recovery. This allows the trade to capture the full Stage 2 advance.

### Trailing Stop
Trail the stop below the rising 30-week moving average. As the MA rises, the stop rises with it. This method keeps the trader in the position through normal pullbacks while automatically exiting when the stock's character changes.

## Common Mistakes

- **Buying in late Stage 2.** When a stock is already 30-40% above its 30-week MA, the risk/reward deteriorates sharply. The best entries are near the Stage 1 breakout or early Stage 2 pullbacks.
- **Ignoring the 30-week MA direction.** A flat or declining 30-week MA means the stock is not in Stage 2 regardless of what the price pattern looks like. The MA must be visibly rising.
- **Not checking the broader market stage.** Individual stocks can break out of Stage 1, but if the S&P 500 is in Stage 4, the failure rate increases dramatically. Market stage acts as a tide.
- **Treating Stage 1 breakouts as Stage 2.** A stock breaking above resistance with a still-flat 30-week MA is a Stage 1 breakout, not Stage 2. Wait for the MA to confirm by turning upward.
- **Using too tight a stop.** Stage 2 trades are inherently intermediate-term. A stop below the 30-week MA may be 8-12% from entry. Position size must account for this wider stop.
