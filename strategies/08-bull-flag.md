# Bull Flag / Pennant

> **Bull flags produce a 67% success rate with an average rise of 23% according to Thomas Bulkowski's pattern research, making them one of the most reliable continuation patterns for swing traders.**

## Overview
- **Author/Origin:** Thomas Bulkowski, *Encyclopedia of Chart Patterns* (2021, 3rd edition)
- **Family:** Momentum / Continuation
- **Direction:** Long
- **Best Market Regime:** Trending Up
- **Typical Hold Time:** 3-8 days

## Why It Works

After a strong upward move (the flagpole), a stock enters a brief consolidation phase where it drifts sideways or slightly downward in a tight channel. This "flag" represents profit-taking by short-term traders and algorithms while institutional buyers hold their positions. The declining volume during the flag confirms that selling pressure is weak -- it is an orderly digestion of gains, not a reversal of conviction.

"Bull flags have a 67% success rate with an average rise of 23%." -- Thomas Bulkowski, *Encyclopedia of Chart Patterns* (2021)

The breakout from the flag works because it resolves the brief supply/demand imbalance in favor of buyers. Short-term sellers have finished exiting, new buyers see the flag as a low-risk entry, and short sellers who faded the flagpole are forced to cover. This triple pressure -- new longs, returning longs, and short covering -- drives the measured move that typically equals the length of the original flagpole.

What distinguishes a high-quality bull flag from a failed one is the volume signature. Volume should decline steadily throughout the flag formation and then surge on the breakout. Bulkowski's research across thousands of patterns showed that flags with volume confirmation on the breakout produced significantly larger post-breakout moves than those without. The flag should also be tight -- retracing less than 50% of the flagpole. Deeper retracements indicate more selling conviction and reduce the probability of a successful continuation.

## Entry Rules

1. A strong prior move up (the flagpole) of at least 10% within 1-3 weeks
2. Tight consolidation forming a downward-sloping or sideways channel (the flag)
3. Volume declines steadily during the flag formation
4. Price remains above the 21-day exponential moving average (EMA21)
5. Flag duration is 5-15 bars -- shorter flags tend to be more powerful
6. Flag retraces less than 50% of the flagpole height
7. Breakout above the flag's upper trendline occurs on a volume surge (1.5x+ average)
8. RSI(14) is between 50-70 on breakout (momentum intact, not overbought)

## Exit Framework

### Stop Loss
Place the stop below the flag low or 1.5x ATR below entry, whichever is tighter. The flag low represents the last point where buyers stepped in -- a break below invalidates the continuation thesis.

### Target 1 (Scale Out)
Measured move: project the flagpole length from the breakout point. Scale out 50% of the position at this level. Bulkowski's data shows the measured move is reached approximately 64% of the time in successful bull flags.

### Target 2 (Remainder)
1.5x the measured move (1.5x flagpole length from breakout). The remaining 50% rides for an extended move, capturing the strongest continuations where momentum feeds on itself.

### Trailing Stop
Once Target 1 is reached, trail the stop below the most recent swing low on the daily chart. This protects profits while allowing the trade room to develop if momentum continues.

## Common Mistakes

- **Flag duration is too long.** A flag that extends beyond 3 weeks is statistically a rectangle pattern, which has different performance characteristics and lower breakout reliability.
- **Flag retraces too deep.** If the flag pulls back more than 50% of the flagpole, selling pressure is stronger than typical for a healthy pause. This increases the failure rate significantly.
- **Low volume on the breakout.** A breakout without a volume surge lacks institutional participation. These "quiet" breakouts fail at a much higher rate than confirmed ones.
- **Confusing a bear flag with a bull flag.** In a downtrend, the same pattern in reverse is a bear flag -- a continuation pattern to the downside. Always confirm the prior trend direction.
- **Not measuring the flagpole for target.** The measured move is the flagpole length projected from the breakout. Without this measurement, traders either exit too early or hold without a plan.
